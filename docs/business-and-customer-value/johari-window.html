# definition

This method shows that "self-perception" and "external perception" do not correspond, but that there are areas of behavior in which others are inadvertently communicated about oneself, while large areas of one's own perception remain hidden. (Stangl, 2020). The Johari window is represented by a quadrant with four main areas. Area A comprises that part of the common knowledge, those aspects of our behavior that are known to ourselves and to the other members of the group.Here we are more or less the 'public person'.Area B describes the 'blind spot', the part of our behavior that we ourselves do not perceive very much, the other members of the group, however, quite clearly: the thoughtless and unconscious habits and behaviors, the prejudices, likes and dislikes.Area C comprises the area of restraint, for example those aspects of our thinking and acting that we consciously hide from others - the 'secret desires', the 'sensitive areas', the so called 'private person'.Area D comprises the unconscious area that is not directly accessible to us or to others; however, a psychologist can find access to him. Hidden talents and untapped talents are examples of this.

# motivation

Quadrants B and D are especially relevant for feedback. Feedback in the sense of a comparison of self-image and external image can, provided the feedback is accepted and reflected constructively, reduce the "unconscious" area. At the same time, the "open" area is enlarged. This can lead to a positive change in relationships with other people, as the effect, i.e. the external image, increases the intentions, i.e. corresponds to the self-image.

# procedure

A participant selects a set number of adjectives from a list they feel best describe themselves.The participant then selects, from the same set of adjectives, the characteristics that best describe another person.You can do this simple exercise in a 30-minute one-on-one meeting, and it can lead to very constructive conversations and achievements.
